Halma on manifolds

  • 2 players: black and white swap places.
  • 3 players: red → green → blue swap cyclically.
  • 4 players: red swaps with blue, green swaps with orange.
  • 6 players: red swaps with blue, green swaps with orange, purple swaps with cyan.
  • 8 players: blacks swaps with white, red swaps with blue, green swaps with orange, purple swaps with cyan.
  • 12 players: blacks swaps with white, red swaps with blue, green swaps with orange, purple swaps with cyan, gold swaps with brown, gray swaps with rainbow.

Torus

In the most common setting, the torus is obtained by taking the usual 16 × 16 board and gluing both the left and right edges and the top and bottom edges to each other without twisting. The same configuration is achieved by starting from an infinite square tiling of the plane, and identifying squares under the group action generated by the two translations (0, 16) and (16, 0) along the horizontal and vertical axis.

Skew torus

As an alternative to the standard torus, one can also consider the infinite square grid and identify squares under the group action generated by translations along the diagonal directions. In order to obtain a board of comparable size, these are chosen as (12, 12) and (12, -12). Then the unit cell has 24 × 12 squares. The torus is obtained by gluing sides to form a "brick wall", i.e., left and right are glued to each other as usual, but top and bottom are shifted by half of the width, i.e., 12 squares before gluing.

Klein bottle

Like the torus, the Klein bottle is obtained by gluing the top and the bottom of the standard 16 × 16 board, as well as the left and the right side after twisting them. For twisting, there are now two possibilities, since the board has no edge leftover: either two ranks meet themselves after twisting, or they meet their neighboring ranks. Here we need the former type, as we will see below, and so we choose the ranks 3 and 11 to meet themselves. Alternatively, the same result is obtained by identifying square of an infinite square grid under the group action generated by a translation (0, 16) and a glide reflection (16, 0), where the mirror axis lies inside the rank 3 (or equivalently rank 11).

Projected cube

The projected cube is obtained from a cube by identifying centrally opposing positions, i.e., positions whose connecting line passes through the center of the cube. It is a non-orientable surface with three faces.
